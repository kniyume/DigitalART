Digital Art Production

Digital art history traces back to the 1960s when artists began experimenting with computers as a medium for creating visual art. One of the pioneers in this field was John Whitney, who used a computer to create abstract animations in the 1960s, laying the foundation for digital animation. The first recognized digital artworks emerged in the 1950s, such as Oscillon 40 (1952) by American mathematician Ben Laposky, who used an oscilloscope to manipulate electronic signals and photograph them in the shape of waves.

Digital art grew out of partnerships between scientific labs and artists eager to explore new technological mediums, contributing to the shaping of today's technologies and their aesthetic qualities. The concept of digital art can be traced back to ideologies present in avant-garde art movements of the first half of the 20th century, such as Modernism, Futurism, Abstraction, and Op art, which responded to the rapid evolution of transport and communications systems and new scientific discoveries.

The first forms of digital art appeared in the 1980s, with roots in early experimentation with digital tools in the 1960s. Digital art encompasses a wide variety of mediums, including computer, generative, robotic, kinetic, and net art, as well as post-internet, virtual reality, and augmented reality art. With the advent of the internet and social media, digital art has become more accessible, allowing artists to share their work with a global audience.
